  1. Item 5: DOM Recycling for the Timeline
   * Benefit: Transformative Performance. This is the difference between a
     "web list" and a "native app" feel. It eliminates the tiny stutters
     (jank) during fast scrolling by keeping the number of DOM nodes constant
     regardless of how many thousands of articles you have.
   * Difficulty: High. Requires a significant refactor of the renderList and
     Virtual Scroll logic to manage a "pool" of elements and update their
     content rather than destroying/creating them.
   * Time Estimate: 4–6 hours.

  2. Item 6: Native-Like Transitions
   * Benefit: Premium UX. On mobile, this makes the app feel "installed."
     Sliding the article view in from the right or fading the timeline creates
     a spatial mental model for the user, making navigation intuitive rather
     than jarring.
   * Difficulty: Medium. Writing the CSS is easy, but coordinating the
     z-index, transform, and state classes (so you don't see content
     "teleporting" behind the transition) requires precision.
   * Time Estimate: 2–3 hours.

  3. Item 8: Remove Redundant Sanitization
   * Benefit: Interaction Speed. Currently, when you click an article, the CPU
     has to work to "clean" the HTML before showing it. Moving this to the
     background (during the initial fetch/save to IndexedDB) makes the
     click-to-read transition feel truly instant and saves battery.
   * Difficulty: Low. Simple logic relocation. We move the sanitization call
     from the "View" layer to the "Data" layer.
   * Time Estimate: 1 hour.

  4. Item 7: Variable Font Integration
   * Benefit: Visual Polish. It allows for much finer control over typography
     (e.g., a weight of 550 for unread titles vs 400 for read) without the
     performance hit of loading multiple font files. It’s purely aesthetic but
     elevates the "Reeder" look.
   * Difficulty: Medium. Requires selecting a high-quality variable font (like
     Inter or Roboto Flex), updating the @font-face configuration, and
     auditing existing CSS font-weight values.
   * Time Estimate: 1.5–2 hours.

✦ Here are the suggested improvements, ordered from most critical impact on Performance, Stability, and User
  Experience to "polish" refinements.

  1. Migrate Storage to IndexedDB (Performance & Stability)
   * Why it's #1: Your PRD mentions a 5,000-link read history. localStorage is synchronous and limited to ~5MB. As
     your history grows, every "Mark as Read" action will block the UI thread while the browser serializes and writes
     that massive string.
   * The Benefit: IndexedDB is asynchronous and has virtually no storage limit. This will eliminate "stutter" during
     navigation and prevent the app from crashing when the data quota is exceeded.

  2. Implement Search Functionality (Utility)
   * Why it's #2: For your "Power Reader" persona with 100+ feeds, finding a specific article is currently impossible
     without scrolling.
   * The Benefit: Since all articles are already in window.articles, a client-side search is extremely fast and
     provides immediate value for triaging content.

  3. Replace Tailwind CDN with Static CSS (Performance)
   * Why it's #3: The Tailwind CDN is meant for prototyping. It injects a ~100KB runtime script that "watches" the
     DOM and generates styles on the fly.
   * The Benefit: Moving to a minified, static <style> block will significantly reduce "First Contentful Paint" time
     and lower the CPU/Memory footprint, especially on older mobile devices.

  4. Fullscreen "Zen Mode" (UX/Design)
   * Why it's #4: The primary goal of an RSS reader is reading. Currently, the sidebar and timeline take up ~50% of
     the desktop screen even when a single article is open.
   * The Benefit: A distraction-free mode (hiding the sidebar and timeline) with a centered text column (70-80ch
     wide) dramatically improves focus and reading speed.

  5. DOM Recycling for the Timeline (Performance)
   * Why it's #5: Your current virtual scroll uses innerHTML = '' followed by appendChild. This forces the browser to
     re-calculate the entire layout of the list pane on every scroll "jump."
   * The Benefit: DOM Recycling (reusing existing card elements and just swapping the text/images) makes scrolling
     butter-smooth, particularly in "Smart Feeds" like All Unread where the list can be long.

  6. Native-Like Transitions (UX)
   * Why it's #6: PWAs often feel "cheap" because they lack the physical cues of native apps.
   * The Benefit: Adding 150ms slide or fade transitions when switching between the Feed list and the Reader on
     mobile makes the app feel "installed" and premium.

  7. Variable Font Integration (Design)
   * Why it's #7: You have multiple font settings for body, title, and metadata. Loading multiple separate font files
     increases network requests and layout shift.
   * The Benefit: A single Variable Font file (like Inter Variable) allows you to fine-tune weights and optical sizes
     for all UI elements with a single request, resulting in much cleaner typography.

  8. Remove Redundant Sanitization (Performance)
   * Why it's #8: You are sanitizing article HTML twice—once when fetching and once when displaying.
   * The Benefit: It's a micro-optimization, but sanitizing once on fetch ensures the "click-to-read" interaction is
     instantaneous, especially for long-form articles.
